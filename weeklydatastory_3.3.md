<h4> Title: Being stronger means you’re likely to live longer, new study finds
    Publisher: The Washington Post
</h4>

<p>
The Washington Post reports on JAMA Network study that shows the correlation between muscular strength and mortality in women between the ages of 63 to 99. The study indicates that women who have more muscular strength were able to live longer compared to women do didn't. The post found that even when controlling for daily physical activity and aerobic fitness, the way to live long is to, "be strong."

JAMA's study looked at women's health, fitness, grip strength, and lifespans to provide insight on how cognition, mobility and functional status and mortality concerns. Previous research had issues distinguishing between fitness, activeness, and strength. Strength, as defined in previous studies, had more to do with flexibility, endurance, and coordination. However, JAMA's study isolated effects of strength and built on the dataset founded by the Woman's Health Initiative. 

JAMA's methodology included the following:
- participants wore activity trackers for the week for objective monitoring
- timed walk-test of aerobic fitness
- multiple medical exams
- lifestyle questionaires
- two checks of their current strength
      - one measured their grip strength with a dynamometer
      - one tested their lower-body strength via a 5-times-sit-to-stand test

JAMA refined their analysis by controlling for people's health, bodily immflammation markers, smoking history, age, ethnicity, other general lifestyle factos, and any historys of flass or use of mobility aids to see if the results held, and it did: strong women survived, and lived longer. 

Michael J. Lamonte, lead author of the study and a professor of epidemiology and healthy aging at the University of Buffalo in New York spoke to the compelling nature of the study. 

"Strength training and endurance exercise, like walking or jogging, have distinct physiological effects, he said, prompting differing changes to our muscles and cells. Together, these effects probably offer our best chance of prolonged health and greater longevity. So, he said, “do both.
