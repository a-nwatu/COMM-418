<h4> Bonus Data Story 1: How Do U.S. Men and WOmen Spend Their Time </h4>

<p>
On March 17th, 2026, Pew Research Center published a story using data from the American Time Use Survey on the daily routines of men and women. The American Time Use Survey (ATUS) is conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau and measures the amount of time people spend doing various activities such as working, child-rearing, extracurricular activities and social activies. 
  
Pew Research Center focused on the average time spent doing twelve different activities: housework, sleeping, eating, spending money, caregiving, working, watching TV, socializing, relaxing, exercising, civic/religious activities and personal grouping. Pew Research Center divided the age groups into six groups: 20s, 30s, 40s, 50s, 60s and 70s+. The data visualization for this article is smart, utilizing a stopwatch to show the differences in behaviors between the genders. At the bottom of the selected category there's a note that talks about the special marker that indicates whether there's a statistically significant difference between the two groups.

The difference between men and women regarding their average time spent daily doing housework is interesting. Men spend an aaverage of an hour and thifty-four minutes, while women spent an average of two hours doing daily housework. This difference can be seen anecdotally through social media, as more and more women have come and shared their experiences on the unequal distribution of maintaining the home. Another statistically significant difference between the two groups is the average time spent caregiving. Women in their 20s - 40s on average spend more time caregiving compared to their male counterparts. However, men across all age groups, on average spend more time working; especially men in their 50s. Men in their 50s spend an average of six hours and fifty-six minutes daily working, while women in that category are only working five hours and twenty-one minutes.

Pew Research Center states that this analysis is a continued effort to understand gender and gender roles, which means researching differences in how each gender is able to spend their time.

Article Link: https://www.pewresearch.org/social-trends/feature/how-do-u-s-men-and-women-spend-their-time/

ATUS Link: https://www.bls.gov/tus/

<h4> Bonus Data Story 2: Key Findings about Black Immigrations in the U.S. </h4>
<p>
On March 20th, Pew Research Center published a report on its Short Reads series presenting key findings about Black immigrants within the United States. From 2000 to 2024, the numver of Black immmigrants have steadily increased thus expanding the diversity in ethnicity within the Black population.

In their methodology section, Pew Research Center uses several federal data sources such as the 2000 Decennial Cenus, 2006/2010/2015/2019/2024 American Community Serveys (ACS), and the 2025 Current Population Survey Annual Social and Economy Supplement (CPS ASE). Pew defines its scope on the Black population by describing it as, "people who are single-race Black, multiracial Black and Black Hispanic."

This data story uses retrospective data to establish trends and better understand a part of the immigrant population within the United States. 

Pew Research Center's top three takeaways are that
1. The Black immigrant population has more than doubled since 2000, growing from 2.4 million in 2000 to 5.6 million in 2024.
   - Black immigrants all make up 11.4% of the total U.S. population and 11.2% of the entire U.S. immigrant population
2. A quarter of Black Americans are immigrants or the children of immigrants
   - Pew Research Center states that atleast 25% of Black people either are immigrants themselves of are U.S.born with at least one immigrant parent.
3. African-born immigrants are the fastest-growing Black immigrant in the U.S.
  - African-born immigrants make up 44% of all Black immigrants in the U.S., rapidly increasing from 24% in 2000


Pew Research Center presents ten updated statistics and observations about Black immigrants within the United States, and includes a note at the end of the article explaining how this article presents updated data than when the article was originally posted in Jan. 2018.


Article Link: https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/03/20/key-findings-about-black-immigrants-in-the-us/
